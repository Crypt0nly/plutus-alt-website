Chapter I

The problem

Somewhere between the work your company sells and the people who do it sits a second, invisible workload: the forwarding, the scheduling, the status updates, the copy-paste between systems that don’t speak to each other. Nobody was hired for it. Everybody does it.

The software industry’s answer has been more platforms — and each new platform adds another inbox, another tab, another place where work waits for a human to move it along. The glue between your tools is people. Expensive, brilliant, distractible people.

Software you operate is overhead. Software that operates for you is leverage.

Chapter II

The system

Plutus is an operating system in the proper sense: a layer that sits above your platforms, schedules the work, and uses each tool for what it is good at. You state a goal in plain language — “prepare the Q3 board pack”, “clean up the open pull requests” — and the system compiles it into concrete, checkable steps, then executes them inside Gmail, Calendar, GitHub, Discord, your files and your business apps.

It rests on four principles:

  1. Plain language is the interface.

    If you can describe the outcome, you can operate the system.

  2. Nothing important without approval.

    Consequential actions halt for a human decision. Always.

  3. The work lands where it belongs.

    In the email thread, the repository, the calendar — not in another inbox.

  4. Receipts after every run.

    What changed, where it changed, and what deserves a second look.

Chapter IV

The proof

Trust in an operating system is earned through control, not promises. Plutus pauses before anything consequential — the email about to leave, the branch about to merge — and waits for your nod. Every run concludes with a full account of itself. And if your work must never leave the building, the system runs on your own machines, entirely on-premises.

Delegation, it turns out, is not about letting go. It is about holding exactly the right things.
